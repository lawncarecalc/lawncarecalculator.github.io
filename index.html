Virginia Cooperative Extension Lawn Care Calculator

An interactive web tool for homeowners in Virginia to calculate fertilizer and lime needs based on soil test results — built from official VCE publications and research.

📖 How to Use This Calculator

This tool accepts soil test reports from Virginia Cooperative Extension (VCE) / Virginia Tech and Waypoint Analytical, and walks you through the same calculations that appear in the original VCE spreadsheet (SPES-40A). Start on the Soil Test Report tab to interpret your results and carry values into the calculators:

1
Get a soil test first. This tool works with reports from Virginia Cooperative Extension (VCE) / Virginia Tech and Waypoint Analytical. On the Soil Test Report tab, select your lab from the dropdown — the rating scales and units will adjust automatically. For VCE reports you'll need the P and K fertility ratings (L–, L, L+, M–, M, M+, H–, H, H+, or VH) and your lime recommendation in lbs/1,000 sq. ft. For Waypoint reports you'll use their rating scale (Very Low, Low, Medium, Optimum, Very High) — these are agronomically equivalent to VCE ratings and are used directly in all calculations without conversion.
2
Choose your tab. Select Cool-Season Lawns (tall fescue, Kentucky bluegrass, fine fescue, perennial ryegrass) or Warm-Season Lawns (bermudagrass, zoysiagrass, centipedegrass, St. Augustinegrass). Use the Lime tab whenever lime is recommended on your soil test.
3
Enter your fertilizer's N-P-K and WIN. The three numbers on the bag give the percentage of nitrogen (N), phosphate (P), and potash (K). If the bag lists Water Insoluble Nitrogen (WIN) under Guaranteed Analysis, enter it in field 5b — this determines whether you follow Program 1 (quickly available N) or Program 2 (slowly available N), each of which has different per-application limits and timing windows.
4
Read your results. The calculator shows number of applications, lbs of fertilizer per application, total bags needed, and the correct timing schedule for your program. Warnings appear if your chosen N rate would exceed the per-application limit for your program.
5
Retest after 3 years. All fertilizer recommendations are valid for up to three growing seasons. Lime recommendations cover a three-year period. After that, have your soil retested.
🧬 Understanding WIN & Nitrogen Programs

The nitrogen source in your fertilizer affects how it should be applied. This calculator uses the program system defined in VCE Soil Test Notes 17 and 18.

⚡ Program 1 — Quickly Available Nitrogen

Fertilizers with less than 15% WIN (water insoluble nitrogen). Includes urea, ammonium sulfate, and calcium nitrate. These release rapidly, so smaller applications are needed more frequently to avoid leaching.

  • Cool-season max: 0.7 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
  • Warm-season max: 0.9 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
  • Timing: fall for cool-season; late spring/summer for warm-season
🌿 Program 2 — Slowly Available Nitrogen

Fertilizers with 15% or more WIN. Includes urea formaldehyde (UF), sulfur-coated urea (SCU), polymer-coated urea (PCU), IBDU, and natural organics. These release over time, reducing leaching risk — preferred on sandy soils.

  • Cool-season max: 0.9 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
  • Warm-season max: 1.0 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
  • Fewer applications needed due to extended release
How to calculate WIN%: WIN% of total N = (WIN lbs listed on bag ÷ Total N%) × 100. For example, a fertilizer with 16% total N and 5.6% WIN has 35% of its nitrogen in slowly available form → Program 2.
🔬 Soil Test Ratings Explained

From Soil Test Note 1 (VCE 452-701): ratings reflect the relative availability of nutrients extracted by the Mehlich-1 method.

RatingMeaningFertilizer Response
L–, L, L+LowPlants almost always respond to fertilizer. Use fertilizer containing that nutrient.
M–, M, M+MediumPlants sometimes respond. Moderate fertilizer recommended to maintain fertility.
H–, H, H+HighPlants usually do not respond. Zero phosphorus or zero potassium fertilizer recommended.
VHVery HighNo response expected. Use nitrogen-only fertilizer; retest next year.

Medium phosphorus = 12–35 lbs/A (Mehlich-1). Medium potassium = 76–175 lbs/A. No soil test is performed for nitrogen — it is too mobile for lab results to be useful.

On fertility ratings and lab comparisons: Waypoint Analytical uses Mehlich 3 extraction; Virginia Tech uses Mehlich-1. These methods extract different amounts of P and K from the same soil, so raw lb/A values are not directly comparable between the two labs. The Commonwealth of Virginia officially addresses this in the Virginia Nutrient Management Standards and Criteria (DCR, revised July 2014), which includes Tables 2-2 and 2-3 providing conversion formulas from Mehlich 3 to Mehlich-1 lb/A values. These conversions are required for certified nutrient management planners producing legally compliant Virginia plans.

However, no conversion is needed for this tool. The lb/A values are not used in any calculations here — only the fertility ratings drive the recommendations and flags. Waypoint calibrates their rating thresholds (Very Low / Low / Medium / Optimum / Very High) to their own Mehlich 3 extraction, just as VCE calibrates their ratings (L / M / H / VH) to Mehlich-1. Both rating systems are set at the same agronomic decision point: the soil concentration at which plant response to fertilization becomes uncertain. A Waypoint "Medium" and a VCE "M" convey the same agronomic meaning to a homeowner — the soil is in a range where fertilizer may or may not produce a response. Entering Waypoint ratings directly into this tool is therefore valid for the purpose of lawn fertilizer and lime decision-making.

Potassium note: The DCR correlation tables show that the Mehlich 3-to-Mehlich-1 conversion factor for K varies significantly by laboratory (ranging from 0.31 to 0.71 depending on the lab). No Waypoint-specific K conversion factor is published in the 2014 document. However, since K ratings — not lb/A values — are what this tool uses, this is not a practical concern for homeowners using this calculator.

Reference: Virginia Department of Conservation and Recreation. Virginia Nutrient Management Standards and Criteria, Revised July 2014. Tables 2-2 and 2-3: Correlation of Soil Analysis Results for Phosphorus and Potassium from Mehlich III to Mehlich I. dcr.virginia.gov
