🌿 Choosing your annual N rate

Your soil test report may include a nitrogen recommendation — use it as a starting point, but verify it against Virginia guidelines. Waypoint reports sometimes recommend up to 4.0 lbs, which exceeds the VCE limit for cool-season grasses.

The maximum you can apply in a single application depends on how quickly your fertilizer releases nitrogen. Quick-release fertilizers — like urea or ammonium sulfate (Program 1: no "Water Insoluble Nitrogen" or WIN listed on the bag) — have a lower per-application limit. Slow-release fertilizers — look for "Water Insoluble Nitrogen (WIN)" on the bag label (Program 2: 15–49% WIN; Program 3: 50% or more WIN) — allow a higher per-application rate and fewer applications per season. If your annual target is 2.5 lbs and you are using a quick-release product (Program 1, max 0.7 lbs per application for cool-season), you will need at least 4 separate applications to stay within Virginia's limits.

Average lawn — tall fescue / bluegrass / ryegrass2.0–2.5 lbs0.7 (P1) / 0.9 (P2) / 1.5 (P3)3

♻ Returning clippings? Recycling grass clippings returns up to one-third of the seasonal nitrogen requirement. If you mulch-mow and leave clippings on the lawn, reduce your chosen total by up to one-third — a lawn targeting 3.0 lbs may only need 2.0 lbs when clippings are consistently returned. (VCE 430-011)

☁️ Sun vs. shade: Grasses in heavily shaded areas need only one-half to two-thirds as much nitrogen as the same grass in full sun. Use the lower end of the range above and consider reducing further — over-fertilizing shaded turf promotes disease rather than growth. Time fall applications after most leaves have fallen so sunlight can reach the grass blades. (VCE 430-011)

☁️ ♻ Both shade and returning clippings? Apply both reductions together. Start with the shade adjustment (reduce your target to one-half to two-thirds), then reduce that result by up to one-third for clippings. For example, a shaded tall fescue lawn targeting 2.5 lbs would become 1.25–1.67 lbs after the shade adjustment, then 0.8–1.1 lbs after the clipping credit — a single modest fall application may meet the full seasonal need. (VCE 430-011)

📅 When to apply — think "SON": September, October, November is the primary window for cool-season grasses. Fall applications build density, root growth, and carbohydrate reserves far more effectively than spring fertilization. A single well-timed fall application can meet many lawns' full needs. (VCE 430-520)

Tall fescue/bluegrass/ryegrass: max 3.5 lbs  |  Fine-leaf fescue: max 2.0 lbs

☀️ Choosing your annual N rate

Your soil test report may include a nitrogen recommendation — use it as a starting point, but verify it against Virginia guidelines. Waypoint rates can reach the upper end of the Virginia range — appropriate for bermudagrass but too high for zoysiagrass or centipedegrass.

The maximum you can apply in a single application depends on how quickly your fertilizer releases nitrogen. Quick-release fertilizers — like urea or ammonium sulfate (Program 1: no "Water Insoluble Nitrogen" or WIN listed on the bag) — have a lower per-application limit. Slow-release fertilizers — look for "Water Insoluble Nitrogen (WIN)" on the bag label (Program 2: 15–49% WIN; Program 3: 50% or more WIN) — allow a higher per-application rate and fewer applications per season. If your annual target is 3.0 lbs and you are using a quick-release product (Program 1, max 0.9 lbs per application for warm-season), you will need at least 4 separate applications to stay within Virginia's limits.

Lawn situation Suggested Annual Application Total Max per application Min. applications
Zoysiagrass / centipedegrass — any situation1.0–1.5 lbs0.7 (P1) / 1.0 (P2/P3)2
Bermudagrass / St. Augustine — average lawn2.0–3.0 lbs0.9 (P1) / 1.0 (P2) / 1.5 (P3)3
Bermudagrass / St. Augustine — higher quality3.0–4.0 lbs0.9 (P1) / 1.0 (P2) / 1.5 (P3)4

♻ Returning clippings? Recycling grass clippings returns up to one-third of the seasonal nitrogen requirement. If you mulch-mow and leave clippings on the lawn, reduce your chosen total by up to one-third. Clippings also contribute organic matter that improves soil structure over time. (VCE 430-011)

☁️ Sun vs. shade: Warm-season grasses in shade need significantly less nitrogen than the same grass in full sun — use the lower end of the range and reduce frequency. Shaded warm-season grasses are also more susceptible to winterkill, so avoid late-season N applications entirely in shaded areas. (VCE 430-011)

☁️ ♻ Both shade and returning clippings? Apply both reductions together. Start with the shade adjustment (reduce your target to one-half to two-thirds), then reduce that result by up to one-third for clippings. A shaded bermudagrass lawn targeting 3.0 lbs would become 1.5–2.0 lbs after the shade adjustment, then 1.0–1.3 lbs after the clipping credit. For shaded zoysia or centipede, the combined result may be well under 1.0 lb annually. (VCE 430-011)

❄️ Potassium and winter hardiness: Potassium is the winterizing nutrient for warm-season grasses. If your soil test rates K as Low, address it in your plan — the benefits of adequate K for winter survival are often more significant for warm-season grasses than for cool-season grasses. If K is already Medium or higher, skip extra K to avoid unnecessary leaching. (VCE 430-520)

📅 When to apply: April 1 through August 15 is the primary window. A modest September application (up to 1 lb water-soluble N per 1,000 sq. ft.) for bermudagrass followed by light applications every 4–6 weeks until frost can extend the growing season and improve winter hardiness — but keep rates conservative and stop before frost. (VCE 430-520)

⛔ Do not fertilize after August 15 unless following the specific fall bermudagrass program above. Late-season nitrogen on warm-season grasses increases winterkill risk.

Bermudagrass/St. Augustine: max 4.0 lbs  |  Zoysiagrass/Centipedegrass: max 2.0 lbs

Virginia Cooperative Extension Lawn Care Calculator

An interactive web tool for homeowners in Virginia to calculate fertilizer and lime needs based on soil test results — built from official VCE publications and research.

📖 How to Use This Calculator

This tool accepts soil test reports from Virginia Cooperative Extension (VCE) / Virginia Tech and Waypoint Analytical. Start on the Soil Test Report tab to interpret your results and carry values into the calculators:

1
Get a soil test first. This tool works with reports from VCE / Virginia Tech and Waypoint Analytical. Select your lab from the dropdown — rating scales and units adjust automatically.
2
Choose your tab. Select Cool-Season Lawns or Warm-Season Lawns and pick your specific grass species — this sets the correct N ceiling and per-application limits. Use the Lime tab whenever lime is recommended. Use Vegetable & Flower Gardens for vegetable or flower beds.
3
Build your application plan. Add up to 4 application slots, each with its own N-P-K grade and WIN%. Different grades per slot let you use a P-correcting fertilizer early in the season and N-only maintenance fertilizers later. The Season Summary tracks cumulative N, P, and K against soil test recommendations.
4
Adjust for clipping recycling. If you mulch-mow and return clippings, reduce your total annual N rate by up to one-third — clippings return significant nitrogen to the lawn. (VCE 430-011)
5
Retest after 3 years. All fertilizer recommendations are valid for up to three growing seasons. Lime recommendations cover a three-year period.
🧬 Understanding WIN & Nitrogen Programs

The nitrogen source in your fertilizer affects how it should be applied. This calculator uses the three-program system defined in VCE Publication 430-011 and Soil Test Notes 17 and 18.

Program 1 — Quickly Available Nitrogen

Fertilizers with less than 15% WIN. Includes urea, ammonium sulfate, and calcium nitrate.

  • Cool-season max: 0.7 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
  • Warm-season max: 0.9 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
Program 2 — Slowly Available Nitrogen

Fertilizers with 15–49% WIN. Includes urea formaldehyde, sulfur-coated urea, polymer-coated urea, IBDU, and natural organics.

  • Cool-season max: 0.9 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
  • Warm-season max: 1.0 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
Program 3 — Majority Slowly Available Nitrogen

Fertilizers with 50% or more WIN. Includes highly processed slow-release products and most natural organics.

  • Cool-season max: 1.0–1.5 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
  • Warm-season max: 1.0–1.5 lb N / 1,000 sq. ft. per application
  • Lower seasonal totals apply (2.0–2.8 lbs cool-season) due to higher per-application rates

Note: Sulfur-coated and polymer-coated urea fertilizers provide slowly available N but the bag label does not list it as WIN. If the fertilizer contains sulfur-coated urea, count that portion as WIN when determining your program. (VCE 430-011)

🔬 Soil Test Ratings Explained

Ratings reflect the relative availability of nutrients in your soil. VCE / Virginia Tech and Waypoint Analytical use different scales, but they are directly comparable — this calculator maps them automatically when you select your report type.

VCE / Virginia Tech scale (Soil Test Note 1, VCE 452-701)

RatingMeaningFertilizer Response
L-, L, L+LowPlants almost always respond. Use fertilizer containing that nutrient.
M-, M, M+MediumPlants sometimes respond. Moderate fertilizer recommended.
H-, H, H+HighPlants usually do not respond. Zero P or zero K fertilizer recommended.
VHVery HighNo response expected. Use nitrogen-only fertilizer; retest next year.

Waypoint Analytical scale (Agronomy Facts 8, Waypoint Analytical 2023)

RatingEquivalent VCE ratingFertilizer Response
Very LowLPlants almost always respond. Use fertilizer containing that nutrient.
LowL / L+Plants likely respond. Use fertilizer containing that nutrient.
MediumMPlants sometimes respond. Moderate fertilizer recommended.
OptimumH-Soil supply is adequate. Maintenance applications only.
Very HighVHNo response expected. Use nitrogen-only fertilizer.

VCE medium phosphorus = 12–35 lbs/A. Medium potassium = 76–175 lbs/A. Waypoint reports nutrients in ppm; VCE reports in lbs/A. The two labs use different extraction methods, but ratings have been calibrated to be directly comparable for Virginia soils. This calculator maps Waypoint ratings to VCE equivalents automatically when you select your report type.
